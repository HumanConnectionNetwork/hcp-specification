# Abstract

This document defines the Correlation Candidate Model of the Humanitarian Connection Protocol (HCP).

A Correlation Candidate is a temporary implementation object generated by an HCP Node after evaluating humanitarian evidence contained within independent Humanitarian Records.

A Correlation Candidate represents a probabilistic humanitarian hypothesis.

It is not a protocol object.

It is never exchanged between HCP Nodes.

Its purpose is to assist humanitarian interpretation by organizing compatible observations before they are presented to users.

The protocol standardizes the semantic role of Correlation Candidates.

It does not standardize how they are generated.

---

# 1. Introduction

Humanitarian emergencies frequently generate multiple independent observations that may describe the same observed Subject.

After evaluating humanitarian evidence, an HCP Node may determine that some Humanitarian Records appear compatible.

Rather than modifying those records, the Node generates a Correlation Candidate.

A Correlation Candidate represents the Node's current interpretation of the available humanitarian evidence.

It is a temporary implementation artifact.

It exists only inside the HCP Node.

The protocol never exchanges Correlation Candidates.

Only Humanitarian Records are exchanged.

---

# 2. Purpose

The purpose of the Correlation Candidate Model is to define the semantic role of Correlation Candidates within HCP.

Correlation Candidates allow implementations to:

- organize compatible observations;
- summarize humanitarian evidence;
- reconstruct humanitarian timelines;
- support explainable correlation;
- prepare humanitarian information for presentation.

Correlation Candidates never replace Humanitarian Records.

They simply represent one possible interpretation of compatible humanitarian evidence.

---

# 3. Design Principles

Every Correlation Candidate follows the fundamental architectural principles of HCP.

---

## Temporary

Correlation Candidates exist only while humanitarian evidence is being evaluated or presented.

They should not be synchronized between Nodes.

---

## Observation-Based

Correlation Candidates represent relationships between Observations.

They never represent verified identities.

---

## Explainable

Every Correlation Candidate should be explainable whenever reasonably possible.

Supporting humanitarian evidence should be available for human interpretation.

---

## Dynamic

Correlation Candidates may change whenever new Humanitarian Records become available.

They should always reflect the Node's current interpretation of available humanitarian evidence.

---

## Implementation Independent

Every HCP Node remains free to define how Correlation Candidates are internally represented.

The protocol defines only their semantic role.

---

# 4. Correlation Candidate Philosophy

A Correlation Candidate is not a humanitarian fact.

It is a humanitarian hypothesis generated from compatible observations.

Its purpose is to assist people during humanitarian emergencies by reducing the number of observations requiring manual evaluation.

Correlation Candidates support humanitarian reasoning.

They never replace human judgment.

The fundamental philosophy of this model is:

**Humanitarian Records preserve observations.**

**Correlation Candidates organize compatible observations.**
---

# 5. Correlation Candidate Structure

The internal structure of a Correlation Candidate remains implementation-specific.

However, every Correlation Candidate conceptually contains:

- a collection of compatible Humanitarian Records;
- a Correlation Score;
- humanitarian evidence supporting the correlation;
- optional humanitarian evidence reducing confidence.

Illustrative representation:

```text
Correlation Candidate

├── Humanitarian Records
├── Correlation Score
├── Supporting Evidence
└── Conflicting Evidence
```

Implementations remain free to organize this information using any internal data structure.

The protocol standardizes only the semantic role of each element.

---

# 6. Associated Humanitarian Records

Every Correlation Candidate references one or more independent Humanitarian Records.

Those records remain immutable.

Correlation never modifies them.

Example:

```text
Correlation Candidate

├── Record A
├── Record B
└── Record C
```

Each Humanitarian Record preserves its original humanitarian observation exactly as reported.

Correlation simply evaluates their compatibility.

---

# 7. Correlation Score

A Correlation Candidate may contain a Correlation Score representing the implementation's estimation of humanitarian compatibility.

Example:

```text
0.92
```

The Correlation Score:

- estimates humanitarian compatibility;
- assists ranking;
- supports human interpretation.

It does not:

- establish identity;
- guarantee correctness;
- replace human verification.

Different implementations may use different scoring systems while preserving semantic compatibility.

---

# 8. Supporting Evidence

Implementations are encouraged to preserve the humanitarian evidence supporting every Correlation Candidate.

Typical supporting evidence includes:

- compatible Reported Label;
- compatible Estimated Age;
- compatible Recognition Features;
- compatible Reported Location;
- compatible Event Type;
- compatible Status;
- compatible reporting source;
- temporal proximity.

Supporting evidence improves explainability.

It assists humanitarian decision-making.

---

# 9. Conflicting Evidence

Implementations are also encouraged to preserve humanitarian evidence reducing correlation confidence.

Examples include:

- incompatible chronology;
- conflicting Recognition Features;
- geographically incompatible observations;
- incompatible Event evolution;
- contradictory humanitarian Status.

Conflicting evidence should not automatically invalidate a Correlation Candidate.

Instead, it contributes additional context supporting transparent human interpretation.

Explainable correlation should expose both supporting and conflicting humanitarian evidence whenever reasonably possible.
---

# 10. Humanitarian Timeline

A Correlation Candidate enables the dynamic reconstruction of a Humanitarian Timeline.

The Timeline is reconstructed by organizing the associated Humanitarian Records according to their observed chronology.

Illustrative example:

```text
Missing Report

        │

        ▼

Rescue

        │

        ▼

Hospital Admission

        │

        ▼

Transfer

        │

        ▼

Family Reunification
```

The Humanitarian Timeline is generated dynamically.

It is never stored as a permanent protocol object.

Different implementations may reconstruct different timelines from the same Humanitarian Records while remaining fully interoperable.

Timeline presentation belongs to **HCP-0015 — Result Presentation Model**.

---

# 11. Lifecycle

A Correlation Candidate exists only while it remains useful for humanitarian interpretation.

Typical lifecycle:

```text
Humanitarian Records

        │

        ▼

Compatibility Evaluation

        │

        ▼

Correlation Candidate

        │

        ▼

Presentation

        │

        ▼

Discarded or Reconstructed
```

Whenever new Humanitarian Records become available, previous Correlation Candidates may be updated, replaced or reconstructed.

Humanitarian Records themselves remain unchanged.

---

# 12. Privacy

Correlation Candidates should contain only the humanitarian information necessary to support humanitarian interpretation.

They should never become permanent identity profiles.

Whenever possible, implementations should prioritize humanitarian evidence over personally identifiable information.

If users voluntarily disclose personal information within Humanitarian Records, such information remains humanitarian evidence.

It never becomes protocol identity.

Additional privacy guidance is defined in:

- **HCP-0021 — Privacy and Data Minimization**

# 14. Summary

The Correlation Candidate Model defines the semantic role of Correlation Candidates within the Humanitarian Connection Protocol.

Correlation Candidates are temporary implementation objects.

They are generated locally by HCP Nodes.

They organize compatible Humanitarian Records.

They assist humanitarian interpretation.

They are never exchanged between Nodes.

Humanitarian Records preserve humanitarian observations.

Correlation Candidates organize compatible observations.

Humanitarian Timelines emerge dynamically.

Humanitarian Cases are generated later for presentation to users.

By separating humanitarian evidence from implementation interpretation, HCP allows Nodes to continuously improve correlation quality without modifying exchanged Humanitarian Records or sacrificing interoperability.

The Correlation Candidate Model reinforces one of the fundamental architectural principles of HCP:

**Humanitarian Records preserve observations.**

**Correlation Candidates organize compatible observations.**

**Humanitarian Cases present humanitarian information.**

**People interpret results.**

**Reality is verified by humans.**
